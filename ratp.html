Task Analysis

In order to reduce the size of the Subway Lines Control rooms of each line of the Paris metro, a study was requested to understand in detail the activity of the traffic controllers and the essential information to display on the giant control panels that ensure safety, fluidity and ponctuality for each subway line.

About the Project

A subway line is controlled by several actors located at different parts of the subway line.
Each of them requires specific information to complete his task but everyone must share the very same Traffic Control Board.

Understanding for each actor precisely what are the tasks involved and the mandatory information required to complete each task was a central activity because a controller error can block a line or put in danger

Activity and Tasks Analysis and Modeling

Task analysis is the analysis of how a task is accomplished, including a detailed description of both manual and mental activities, task and element durations, task frequency, task allocation, task complexity, environmental conditions, necessary clothing and equipment, and any other unique factors involved in or required for one or more people to perform a given task.

Information from a task analysis can then be used for many purposes, such as personnel selection and training, tool or equipment design, procedure design (e.g., design of checklists, or decision support systems) and automation. Though distinct, task analysis is related to user analysis.

Wireframe 1: The information available on the TCO excluding incidents

 

In general conditions of use, we must find everything relating to the regulation of navigation.

It is therefore a question of representing the tracks, signaling and trains.

=> Several informations are hidden until these will pop in alert boxes when required.

Wireframe 2: Information available during a specific regulation

 

When regulation event happens, related information appears  (and disappears when obsolete).

This will ease focus on elements representing modifications of Traffic Signals such as traffic lights or distance between trains.


As soon as an unexpected event appears, it is displayed at the bottom of the screen as an alert that will remain until controller treats the incident.

The impacts of my work

In addition with other technical studies performed by other engineers, the study helped RAPT (Paris Subway Company) defining the future of their Control Centers and Opened enhancement points to help the Traffic Controllers focus their attention on the most important information.

An example of the results provided by our work is available on Louvres Station (Line 1) where an information panel explains how the Control center of the line works.
